\chapter*{General Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Introduction}
\markboth{General Introduction}{}

\hspace{0.8cm}Video surveillance systems are now widely deployed in shops, schools, offices, warehouses, residential buildings, and public spaces. Yet many installations still rely on fragmented tools for live viewing, recording, incident search, and user access management. In practice, this means delayed reaction time, difficult remote supervision, and limited operational value from cameras that are already in place.

\hspace{0.8cm}\textbf{VigileEye -- Intelligent Video Surveillance System} was developed in this context during our final year internship at IBSOFT. The objective of the project was to design and implement a unified platform that centralizes camera management, live monitoring, recording, collaborative sharing, alerts, analytics, and AI-assisted security functions around existing IP-camera infrastructure. The proposed solution aims to answer a real operational need while remaining extensible for later infrastructure hardening and deployment maturation.

\hspace{0.8cm}This report is organized as follows. \textbf{Chapter I} presents the project context, the study of existing solutions, the requirements, the chosen methodology, and the main architectural orientations. \textbf{Chapter II} details Sprint I, dedicated to camera management, streaming, recording, and storage. \textbf{Chapter III} covers Sprint II, focused on intelligent monitoring, alerts, analytics, AI-assisted querying, and subscription management. \textbf{Chapter IV} presents Sprint III, devoted to authentication, multi-factor security, invitations, and collaborative access management. \textbf{Chapter V} documents Sprint IV, which concerns the preparation, fine-tuning, export, and integration of the clip-level event-detection model.

\subsection*{Introduction}

\subsection{Project Presentation}

\subsubsection{Challenges}
\hspace{0.8cm}Managing a surveillance deployment at any real scale turns out to be harder than most people expect. It's not simply a matter of having cameras in place — it's everything that needs to work at the same time: juggling multiple streaming protocols, dealing with bandwidth constraints, figuring out where video should be stored and how long it should stick around, and controlling who gets to see which feed. Legacy setups can handle individual pieces of this puzzle, but they were never designed to handle all of them at once in a system that's flexible and accessible from anywhere.

\hspace{0.8cm}Two issues in particular kept surfacing in our thinking. The first was latency. If you're monitoring a security feed from a different city, you can't be watching footage that's three seconds behind reality. At that point you're not monitoring — you're reviewing history. The second was storage. Continuous recording across a few dozen cameras generates a staggering volume of data, and if you don't have smart retention policies combined with the freedom to pick your storage backend on a per-camera basis, you either fill up your disks or you're stuck with whatever pricing model a single cloud provider imposes. We kept circling back to these two problems throughout the design phase.

\subsubsection{Current System Assessment}
\hspace{0.8cm}We conducted a thorough review of what the market already offered before writing a single line of code. Three categories of existing solutions stood out as representative of the landscape.

\hspace{0.8cm}Plenty of deployments still run on physical DVR or NVR hardware, and to be fair, for a single-site setup with basic requirements, they do the job. But the moment you want remote access, multi-site visibility, or any kind of intelligent alerting, you start running into walls. You're setting up port forwarding, wrestling with dynamic IP addresses, and hoping that the manufacturer's mobile app is still getting security patches. It works, in a limited sense, but it doesn't scale well toward connected, collaborative, and analytics-driven use cases [29].

\subsubsection{Criticism of the Existing System}
\hspace{0.8cm}After examining all three categories, a few recurring weaknesses became hard to ignore:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Vendor Lock-in:} Commercial solutions bind you to their proprietary hardware or cloud ecosystem, making it costly and painful to switch when your needs evolve.
  \item \textbf{Limited Remote Access:} Achieving dependable remote viewing usually involves opening ports and configuring VPNs — steps that bring security risks most end users aren't prepared to handle.
  \item \textbf{No Collaborative Sharing:} None of the solutions we evaluated offer native support for granting camera access to specific individuals through a permission-based system. It's an all-or-nothing proposition.
  \item \textbf{Rigid Storage Options:} You're generally committed to a single storage backend. Migrating away from it means moving everything.
  \item \textbf{High Configuration Barrier:} Open-source tools demand serious technical expertise to deploy and maintain. That's fine for power users, but it shuts out a huge potential audience.
  \item \textbf{No Detection Zones:} Very few platforms allow you to draw custom monitoring regions on a live video feed, assign severity levels to them, and schedule when they should be active.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Project Goals}
\hspace{0.8cm}Drawing from everything we'd observed, we organized the objectives of VigileEye into three complementary categories:

\textbf{Functional objectives}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Build a web-based platform for centralized IP-camera management, supporting RTSP, HTTP, RTMP, ONVIF, and HLS protocols.
  \item Provide collaborative camera sharing through invitations, permission levels, and group-management features.
  \item Deliver live viewing, recording, storage management, and polygon-based detection zones with scheduling and alert severity.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Technical objectives}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Implement a robust authentication stack combining JWT sessions, two-factor authentication via email OTP, and Google OAuth.
  \item Ensure real-time video delivery through WebRTC, with HTTP JPEG polling as a transparent fallback when negotiation cannot complete.
  \item Keep the platform modular through a microservice-oriented decomposition, Clean Architecture inside backend services, and a cross-platform frontend covering web and mobile.
  \item Support flexible recording persistence by allowing local filesystem, MinIO, or Azure Blob Storage backends on a per-camera basis.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{AI-oriented objectives}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Provide object-level intelligent monitoring for live intrusion workflows inside the main platform.
  \item Build a dedicated anomaly-classification pipeline that fine-tunes video backbones on a unified surveillance dataset and exposes asynchronous inference through a separate Event Detection service.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Solution Overview}
\hspace{0.8cm}We structured the platform as a set of microservices, each with a well-defined responsibility. The \textbf{Authentication Service} manages everything related to user identity: registration, email verification, two-factor authentication, Google OAuth, password management, and the complete JWT token lifecycle. The \textbf{Members Service} owns the sharing layer — handling individual invitations, group creation, bulk invitations, and permission management.

\hspace{0.8cm}The \textbf{Camera Management Service} covers each camera's full lifecycle: creation, configuration, health monitoring, and detection zone management with scheduling capabilities. The \textbf{Streaming Service} (Go) ingests RTSP streams through FFmpeg, pushes them to MediaMTX, and delivers WebRTC feeds to viewers using WHEP. The \textbf{Storage Service} (Go) handles segmented MP4 recording and uses the Strategy Pattern to abstract the storage backend. Sprint II expands the platform with five supporting services: \textbf{Object Detection} (YOLO + tracking) for intrusion events and snapshots, \textbf{Notification} (FCM + delivery logging) for push alerts with email fallback, \textbf{Analytics} for dashboard aggregations (cache-assisted), \textbf{AI Chatbot} (LangChain) for natural-language SQL analytics over detections, and \textbf{Subscription} (Stripe integration) for plan gating and billing history. A later AI-focused sprint adds a dedicated \textbf{Event Detection Service}, deployed separately on DigitalOcean, responsible for clip-level anomaly classification through fine-tuned video backbones, asynchronous inference workers, and model export for production serving. On the frontend side, a single React/TypeScript application built with \textbf{Ionic} and \textbf{Capacitor} serves as the control center across web and mobile.

\subsubsection{Agile Methodology: Scrum}
\hspace{0.8cm}Scrum revolves around short, fixed-length iterations — called sprints — typically lasting one to four weeks, with each one wrapping up with something tangible you can demonstrate. It defines three roles: Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Development Team. There are four ceremonies: sprint planning, daily standup, sprint review, and retrospective [5]. The framework sounds straightforward on paper, but in practice it imposes a discipline that keeps everyone accountable for what's actually been accomplished versus what's still pending. Problems get raised early. Objectives stay front and center. Any scope creep has to justify itself against a defined sprint boundary before it can slip in.

\subsubsection{Why Scrum}
\hspace{0.8cm}Once we laid it all out, the answer was fairly clear. VigileEye spans multiple microservices written in two different languages, and the requirements shifted as we learned more about what WebRTC and the storage subsystem actually needed in practice. XP has solid engineering practices at its core, but it doesn't offer the backlog structure or role clarity needed to coordinate a multi-service project with an external stakeholder involved. For what we were building, Scrum struck the right balance.

\subsubsection{Actors Identification}
\hspace{0.8cm}Three primary actor profiles interact with the system: the \textbf{Camera Owner}, the \textbf{Reader}, and the \textbf{Writer}. The Camera Owner initiates camera sharing, while the Reader and Writer correspond to the two permission modes granted to accepted shared users.

\textbf{Camera Owner:} The primary user who registers cameras, sets up detection zones, controls streaming, recording, and storage, and shares access with others. Camera Owners hold administrative control over their resources.

\textbf{Reader:} A shared user with read-only access to the cameras that were explicitly granted by a Camera Owner. Readers can watch live streams, browse shared recordings, consult alerts and analytics within their authorization scope, and receive shared notifications.

\textbf{Writer:} A shared user with editor-level access on the cameras that were explicitly granted by a Camera Owner. Writers keep all Reader capabilities and may additionally modify shared camera settings and operational configuration within the granted scope.

\textbf{Generic user scope:} In a few authentication-related flows, the report still uses the generic term \textbf{User} when an action applies to every registered account regardless of whether that account currently acts as a Camera Owner, Reader, or Writer.

\subsubsection{Non-Functional Requirements}
\hspace{0.8cm}Beyond what the system does, there are expectations around how well it does it. These weren't areas where we had room to compromise.

\textbf{Security:} Every access point is protected by JWT tokens paired with two-factor authentication and Google OAuth. Passwords are hashed with bcrypt using twelve salt rounds — not ten, twelve, because we tested it and the marginal cost in time was worth the added strength. After three consecutive failed login attempts, the account gets locked for an hour. Role-based access control ensures that invited members can't perform actions beyond what they were explicitly authorized for.

\textbf{Performance:} The platform aims for sub-second end-to-end latency on live WebRTC streams served through MediaMTX. For situations where WebRTC negotiation can't complete — common for users behind restrictive firewalls or double NAT — HTTP JPEG polling provides a configurable-interval fallback.

\textbf{Ergonomics:} The React frontend is fully responsive across desktop, tablet, and mobile screen sizes. The Ionic + Capacitor app delivers a mobile experience on both iOS and Android using the same codebase, so monitoring isn't confined to a desk.

\textbf{Scalability:} Each microservice can be scaled horizontally on its own. The storage service uses the Strategy Pattern specifically so that the backend can be swapped on a per-camera basis without touching any of the recording logic.

\textbf{Reliability:} Retention policies execute automatically so storage doesn't silently fill up. Camera health monitoring catches connectivity problems before they snowball. When WebRTC fails, the system degrades gracefully to HTTP polling rather than showing the user a blank screen.

\subsection{Project Architecture}
\label{subsec:architecture}

\hspace{0.8cm}Three architectural patterns govern VigileEye at different levels of the system. Clean Architecture operates at the individual service level, keeping business logic isolated from frameworks and persistence concerns [1]. MVVM organizes the React frontend, and a microservice-oriented decomposition structures the system as a whole [2]. Each pattern was chosen to solve a specific problem at a specific scale, not simply to accumulate fashionable terminology.

\subsubsection{How the Architectural Choices Fit Together}
\hspace{0.8cm}The three choices are complementary rather than competing. At the \textbf{global system level}, VigileEye is decomposed into services with distinct responsibilities such as authentication, camera management, streaming, storage, detection, notification, analytics, and subscription management. At the \textbf{internal service level}, each backend keeps its domain and use cases separated from infrastructure through Clean Architecture. At the \textbf{frontend level}, MVVM helps structure pages, stateful hooks, and API-service modules. In short: microservices explain how the platform is split, Clean Architecture explains how each backend is built internally, and MVVM explains how the user interface is organized.

\needspace{6\baselineskip}
\subsubsection{Clean Architecture}
\hspace{0.8cm}Robert C.\ Martin's Clean Architecture organizes code into concentric layers where dependencies only ever flow inward. Outer layers depend on inner ones, never the other way around [1]. We applied this principle to every backend service, using four distinct rings.

\textbf{Domain Layer (innermost):} Pure business logic — entities, value objects, repository interfaces, domain service interfaces. No framework import belongs here, period.

\textbf{Application Layer:} Use cases that orchestrate domain objects to carry out specific user intentions, plus DTOs that keep the API's data representation separate from the internal model.

\textbf{Infrastructure Layer:} Concrete implementations of the interfaces defined in the inner layers — SQLAlchemy repositories for the Python services, Go repository implementations, SMTP integrations, JWT tooling, bcrypt hashing.

\textbf{API Layer (outermost):} FastAPI route handlers or Gin HTTP handlers that parse incoming requests, invoke use cases through injected dependencies, and serialize responses.

\subsubsection{Microservices Architecture}
\hspace{0.8cm}VigileEye adopts a \textbf{microservice-oriented decomposition} in which business capabilities are split into distinct services: Auth, Members Invitation, Camera Management, Streaming, Storage, Object Detection, Notification, Analytics, AI Chatbot, Subscription, and the separate Event Detection service. In the implemented platform, Traefik acts as the public ingress and reverse-proxy layer, while backend services expose well-defined HTTP APIs behind it. This organization isolates heavy workloads --- especially streaming, recording, and AI inference --- from CRUD-oriented business services and reduces direct coupling between domains.

\hspace{0.8cm}At the same time, the current platform should not be presented as a fully industrialized microservice ecosystem yet. It already provides service separation, Docker-based packaging, JWT-based trust propagation, and logical database isolation, but some capabilities remain partial or future-facing: centralized observability, distributed tracing, dynamic service discovery, and stronger platform-wide gateway governance. For that reason, the most accurate formulation is that VigileEye \textbf{implements a microservice-oriented architecture whose supporting infrastructure is still being consolidated}.

\hspace{0.8cm}Figure~\ref{fig:microservices} summarizes that implemented service landscape more faithfully than the earlier draft: an AWS-hosted core platform, service-owned persistence boundaries, specialized real-time media delivery through MediaMTX, and a separate DigitalOcean event-detection runtime reached through authenticated cross-cloud calls.

\LandscapeDiagram{\PathCaseStudy{microservices_Arch.png}}{VigileEye Microservices Architecture}{fig:microservices}

\subsubsection{Inter-Service Communication Model}
\hspace{0.8cm}The services do not all communicate in the same way; the communication style depends on the responsibility being carried:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Synchronous REST/HTTP calls:} used for CRUD operations, permission checks, camera metadata access, subscription checks, notification triggers, and analytics retrieval.
  \item \textbf{WebSocket delivery:} used by the Object Detection service to push live alert and event updates to the frontend dashboard.
  \item \textbf{WebRTC/WHEP and RTSP media flows:} used in the streaming path, where MediaMTX handles the media plane while backend services remain on the control plane.
  \item \textbf{Asynchronous task processing:} used inside the Event Detection service, where FastAPI accepts the request, Redis brokers the task, and a Celery worker processes the clip asynchronously.
\end{itemize}

\hspace{0.8cm}This distinction is important architecturally. Business coordination between services is mostly synchronous and API-based, whereas latency-sensitive media delivery and long-running clip classification are deliberately handled through specialized protocols or asynchronous workers.

The Event Detection service is the main exception: it keeps its own PostgreSQL store beside its DigitalOcean runtime because its asynchronous inference lifecycle has a different operational profile.

\subsubsection{Frontend Architecture: MVVM}
\hspace{0.8cm}The React frontend follows a Model--View--ViewModel organization. The \textbf{Model} consists of TypeScript interfaces and API service modules that make HTTP requests. The \textbf{ViewModel} is custom hooks — \texttt{useAllCameras}, \texttt{useWebRTCStream}, \texttt{useWHEPStream}, \texttt{useAudioStream} — that manage state, handle side effects, and run the WebRTC connection lifecycle, including the automatic fallback to HTTP polling when WebRTC can't connect [8][9][10].

\subsubsection{Backend Service Architectures}

\textbf{FastAPI Services (Python backends):} The Python services are built with FastAPI using SQLAlchemy as the ORM, Alembic for schema migrations, and Pydantic for request validation [6][16]. \textbf{Go Services (Streaming \& Storage):} The Go services use the Gin HTTP framework. The Streaming service coordinates FFmpeg as the camera ingestor, MediaMTX as the stream relay, and the \texttt{pion/webrtc} library for WebRTC negotiation [7][11][12][13]. \hspace{0.8cm}In practice, these measurements supported two conclusions. First, the target of sub-second live delivery was realistic with the selected stack. Second, separating the media plane through MediaMTX improved both reconnect behavior and concurrent-stream capacity. When a browser cannot complete ICE negotiation for WebRTC, the system silently switches over to HTTP JPEG polling, which proved especially useful during development behind double NAT and restrictive local-network conditions.

\hspace{0.8cm}The storage service's Strategy Pattern implementation made backend switching completely invisible to the recording logic. The same goroutine that writes MP4 segments hands them off to a \texttt{StorageBackend} interface, and the concrete implementation --- local disk, MinIO, or Azure Blob --- is determined at configuration time. Retention policies run as scheduled background tasks, automatically cleaning up segments older than the configured window.

\hspace{0.8cm}Sprint I was also supported by automated validation suites across the three concerned services. In the current repository snapshot, \textbf{Camera Management} reports \textbf{26 unit + 16 integration tests} with \textbf{42 passed}, \textbf{Streaming} reports \textbf{51 unit + 16 integration tests} with the unit suite passing and the integration package currently blocked by a middleware-signature mismatch, and \textbf{Storage} reports \textbf{27 unit tests}, all passed. \section{Sprint I Retrospective}
\label{sec:s1_retrospective}

\textbf{What Went Well:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The Go + MediaMTX stack achieved sub-second WebRTC latency from the very first end-to-end test. That early success gave us real confidence in the architecture at a point when everything else still felt uncertain.
  \item The Strategy Pattern for storage backends was one of the cleanest design decisions across the whole project. Getting Azure support working after MinIO was already in place took a single afternoon — one new struct, one interface implementation, and we were done.
  \item FFmpeg handled ONVIF, RTSP, and HTTP streams without us needing to write protocol-specific code paths. Its resilience when faced with non-standard stream configurations honestly impressed us.
  \item Integration tests caught two retention policy bugs that would've been almost impossible to track down by hand — specifically, edge cases triggered by recordings created through concurrent inserts from two different services.
  \item The detection zone canvas, despite being the most technically novel UI component we built, was received well during the sprint review and sparked some good conversation about how AI detection could plug into it later.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What Didn't Go Well:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Error propagation and timeout handling across three services communicating with each other proved far more complex than we'd planned for. We spent a full day hammering out a consistent error response format after noticing inconsistencies during integration testing — that conversation should've happened earlier.
  \item MediaMTX ICE negotiation required careful STUN configuration tuning, and trying to reproduce NAT traversal failures in a local development environment was tedious and not always reliable.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Improvements for Future Development:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Strengthen gateway policies around the existing Traefik ingress so cross-service routing, timeout handling, and centralized logging become more consistent.
  \item Deploy a TURN server to improve WebRTC reliability for users behind restrictive corporate firewalls — currently our main connectivity failure scenario.
  \item Build automated end-to-end tests covering the full path from FFmpeg ingestion through to browser playback, so regressions across the streaming pipeline get caught early.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Conclusion}

\enlargethispage{10\baselineskip}
\hspace{0.8cm}Sprint I was the most technically demanding phase we went through, but it was also the most rewarding. Watching a live camera feed appear in the browser — routed through FFmpeg, relayed by MediaMTX, and delivered over a WebRTC connection negotiated by our own code — confirmed that the architectural decisions made earlier were sound. The cross-service integration challenges were tough, yet working through them gave us a much sharper understanding of how distributed systems behave under real conditions. This sprint transformed VigileEye from a secure but hollow authentication framework into a functional, observable, and configurable surveillance platform.

\hspace{0.8cm}\textbf{Sprint goal:} Deliver intelligent monitoring workflows and monetize premium capabilities without compromising operational reliability.

\section{Realisation of Sprint IV}
\label{sec:s4_realisation}

\textbf{Dataset Consolidation and Label Governance}

\hspace{0.8cm}The first implementation challenge was not model training but data consistency. Each public dataset came with its own file organization, anomaly vocabulary, and annotation conventions. We therefore treated label harmonization as a first-class engineering task. The shared schema grouped all inputs into thirteen target classes --- including \texttt{Normal}, \texttt{Assault/Fight}, \texttt{Burglary}, \texttt{Robbery}, \texttt{Explosion}, \texttt{RoadAccident}, and \texttt{Vandalism} --- and every cleaner was required to emit the same metadata columns before a sample could enter the master CSV. This prevented silent label drift across experiments and made the evaluation reports interpretable across dataset boundaries.

\textbf{Fine-Tuning Strategy and Model Selection}

\hspace{0.8cm}Once the corpus was stabilized, we focused on reproducible fine-tuning. The training driver supports multiple backbones behind one configuration contract, which let us compare SlowFast and VideoMAE under the same split logic, transform policy, and evaluation protocol. Checkpointing, early stopping, and post-training evaluation were not treated as optional conveniences; they were core controls designed to keep the sprint honest. In an imbalanced anomaly-classification problem, accuracy alone can be deeply misleading, so the pipeline emphasizes F1-oriented monitoring and exported confusion matrices before a checkpoint is promoted. The result is a training workflow that behaves like a real service pipeline, not like a one-off notebook experiment.

\textbf{Cross-Cloud Runtime Contract}

\hspace{0.8cm}The operational boundary between DigitalOcean and AWS was defined through a deliberately small interface. The validated and implemented entry points are \texttt{/predict}, \texttt{/events}, \texttt{/status}, and \texttt{/health}; the websocket route \texttt{/ws/alerts} already exists at the API level, but in the current codebase it remains a prepared extension point rather than the primary production path. The communication model adopted in the report relies on four assumptions, each aligned with the repositories:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The Authentication service issues JWTs signed with the same shared secret and algorithm that the Event Detection service validates.
  \item AWS-side modules perform permission, membership, and subscription checks before forwarding a classification request to DigitalOcean.
  \item Only the FastAPI ingress is exposed cross-cloud over HTTPS; Redis and PostgreSQL remain private to the DigitalOcean deployment boundary.
  \item Result retrieval is asynchronous: the caller receives a task identifier immediately, then retrieves the persisted event through \texttt{/events} or a future live-push channel.
\end{itemize}

\hspace{0.8cm}Within that contract, the surrounding VigileEye modules interact with the AI service in different ways. Authentication provides the trust anchor. Members Management and Subscription participate upstream by deciding who may invoke premium AI operations. Streaming and Storage provide clip sources and persistent evidence paths. Notification consumes the resulting events for alert delivery. The AI Chatbot and analytics layer reuse the classified-event data downstream for query answering and reporting. Object Detection remains complementary rather than redundant: it handles object-level intrusion logic in live frames, whereas the Event Detection service focuses on clip-level anomaly classification over curated video segments.

\hspace{0.8cm}One additional precision point deserves emphasis. The runtime database technology used by the Event Detection service is \textbf{PostgreSQL}, as shown by the service configuration, ORM models, and migration setup. Redis is used strictly as a Celery broker and result backend. The cleaned \textasciitilde110 GB training corpus is neither a PostgreSQL database nor a Redis dataset; it is a filesystem-resident video corpus with CSV indexes that feeds the training pipeline only.

\section{Sprint IV Retrospective}
\label{sec:s4_retrospective}

\textbf{What Went Well:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The unified label schema prevented hidden taxonomy drift when combining six heterogeneous surveillance datasets.
  \item Keeping training, export, and serving concerns separate made the AI work easier to reason about and much easier to document.

\begin{itemize}
  \item The cleaned corpus was large enough to make preprocessing and repeated experimentation I/O-heavy.
  \item Public anomaly datasets do not share one stable semantic vocabulary, which made label harmonization more time-consuming than expected.
  \item The worker still needs its final ONNX Runtime inference hook before the runtime path can be considered fully operational.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Improvements for Future Iterations:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Complete the worker-side ONNX Runtime execution path and benchmark it on the target DigitalOcean hardware profile.
  \item Add contract tests covering AWS-to-DigitalOcean request signing, retry behavior, and result retrieval.
  \item Introduce experiment tracking and drift monitoring so retraining decisions can be triggered by production evidence rather than by manual inspection alone.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Conclusion}

\hspace{0.8cm}Sprint IV gave VigileEye something more durable than a single trained model: it established an AI lifecycle. The project now includes a governed training corpus, a reproducible fine-tuning pipeline, exportable runtime artefacts, and a clearly defined cross-cloud service boundary. That combination is what turns AI from a demonstration feature into an engineering subsystem that the rest of the platform can evolve around.

\chapter*{General Conclusion}

\markboth{General Conclusion}{}

\hspace{0.8cm}Four months. A multi-service platform. Four sprints. One project that started as a brief from a software company in Mahdia and wound up being the most complete piece of engineering work either of us has produced. That's the simplest way to sum up VigileEye — but it doesn't quite capture what the experience actually felt like from the inside.

\hspace{0.8cm}The system we built is real. It manages user identity through a full authentication stack that combines JWT sessions, two-factor verification via email OTP, and Google OAuth. It lets Camera Owners share access with individuals or groups through a structured, permission-aware invitation system. It ingests RTSP streams from physical IP cameras, routes them through FFmpeg and MediaMTX, and delivers sub-second WebRTC feeds to browsers and mobile devices, with HTTP polling as a transparent fallback when WebRTC negotiation can't complete. It records video in configurable MP4 segments and writes them to local storage, MinIO, or Azure Blob depending on the owner's configuration, with retention policies that clean up automatically. Detection zones can be drawn as polygons on live frames, assigned severity levels and schedules, and consumed by two complementary intelligence layers: an object-level intrusion service inside the main platform and a dedicated clip-level Event Detection service fine-tuned on curated surveillance datasets and prepared for cross-cloud deployment on DigitalOcean. The whole thing runs as independent microservices following Clean Architecture — each one testable in isolation, each one deployable on its own, none of them tied to a particular framework or infrastructure vendor.

\hspace{0.8cm}If we look at the technical choices in hindsight, we'd make most of them again. FastAPI for the Python services, because the async performance and Pydantic integration genuinely accelerated our development. Go for streaming and storage, because the benchmarks were unambiguous and the goroutine model fit the problem perfectly. React and TypeScript for the frontend (Ionic + Capacitor), because we knew them well and the MVVM pattern with custom hooks worked cleanly. Neon for shared PostgreSQL, because developing against a single live schema from two different machines eliminated a whole class of bugs. Scrum for project management, because sprint boundaries kept us honest and the retrospectives actually changed the way we worked — Sprint II's approach was noticeably different from Sprint I based on what we'd learned from the first retrospective.

\hspace{0.8cm}What we didn't fully expect was how much the non-technical side of the work would end up teaching us. Coordinating shared infrastructure, reviewing each other's pull requests, debugging a failure that turned out to span three services and two different languages, explaining an architectural decision to a supervisor who hadn't seen the codebase — those were the real lessons of this internship. The code matters, of course. But knowing how to function as engineers in a professional setting, communicate clearly about technical problems, and keep pushing forward when things don't work the way they're supposed to — that's what the four months were ultimately about.

\hspace{0.8cm}We're proud of VigileEye. It works as a functional multi-service platform, it is supported by substantial automated validation suites, and it is built to be extended.

\subsection*{Perspectives}

\hspace{0.8cm}The most immediate next step is closing the last operational gap in the AI serving path: wiring the Event Detection worker to execute the exported ONNX model directly in production and benchmarking that path on the target DigitalOcean instance. From there, the more interesting problem is feedback-driven improvement. The platform now has enough structure to collect validated incidents, operator acknowledgements, and curated evidence clips that could feed a future retraining loop rather than leaving model improvement as a purely manual exercise. The Ionic + Capacitor application already covers web and mobile targets, but a dedicated cycle could still deepen offline handling, improve background notification behavior, and close any remaining platform-specific gaps.

\hspace{0.8cm}On the infrastructure side, deploying a TURN server would meaningfully improve WebRTC reliability for users behind corporate firewalls or carrier-grade NAT — that's currently the most common connectivity failure scenario we're aware of. Looking further ahead, moving object detection inference to an edge device co-located with the cameras would cut both latency and cloud bandwidth. Edge hardware is becoming capable enough for this faster than we expected when we started. These directions would take VigileEye well past a final year project and toward a more broadly deployable platform.
